Stony Creek Water Quality Analysis (2002–2021)

Ilianna Ditren, Natalie Novoseltseva, Joy Terentiev

Introduction

Project Background

  • Focus area: Stony Creek, NY
  • Initial analysis covered 2020–2021 data
  • Original project analyzed:
    • Temperature
    • pH
    • Salinity

Our Contributions

  • Expanded analysis to all available years (2002-2021)
  • Combined water quality data with weather data
  • Explored:
    • Long-term trends
    • Weather–water relationships
    • Seasonal patterns
    • Anomalies and possible data errors

Research Question

How did water quality indicators change over time, and were these changes associated with weather conditions?

  • Exploratory analysis only
  • First step toward studying climate-related environmental change

What the Report Includes

  • Data cleaning and preparation
  • Exploratory analysis
  • Visualizations
  • Seasonal analysis

Data and Methods

Data Sources

  • Stony Creek water quality data (2002–2021)

  • Hudson Field Station weather data (2001–2026)

  • Data source: Centralized Data Management Office (CDMO) https://cdmo.baruch.sc.edu

Data Cleaning

  • Removed empty rows and columns

  • Separated data and time column and added a year, month and season column

  • Removed flagged data and then removed columns

Final Dataset

  • combined: Creates one dataset by joining water quality data from Stony Creek and meteorological data from Hudson Field Station.

Main Variables

Column Description
Turb Turbidity measured in units referred to as FNU/NTU, depending upon sensor.
TotPrcp total precipitation measured in millimeters (mm)
SpCond Specific conductivity measured in milli-Siemens per centimeter (mS/cm)

Conclusion

  • Several data quality issues were identified, including missing values and possible sensor errors.
  • Some plots suggest higher temperatures in recent years, especially during summer, but no clear long-term warming trend was confirmed.
  • Interesting relationships were observed, particularly between precipitation and water temperature.
  • Improved approaches were identified, including averaging data by day or week and analyzing seasons separately.

Next Steps

  • Extend the analysis beyond exploratory methods by applying statistical modeling and time-series analysis.
  • Analyze extreme weather events separately to better understand their environmental impacts and associated water quality changes.
  • Improve data quality assessment by identifying sensor anomalies and patterns of missing data.
  • Develop predictive models to better understand how water quality responds to precipitation and seasonal weather conditions.
